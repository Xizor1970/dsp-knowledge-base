1. Sampling & Aliasing

See Aliasing Happen

What you're seeing

The blue curve is the original continuous signal. Red dots are samples taken at the chosen rate. The dashed green curve shows what frequency is reconstructed from those samples. When sample rate < 2× signal frequency, the green curve shows a DIFFERENT frequency — that's aliasing.

So what?

This demonstrates the Nyquist theorem viscerally. Push the signal frequency above half the sample rate and watch the reconstructed signal become something entirely different. This is irreversible — once aliased, the original frequency is unrecoverable. Every ADC in existence must prevent this.

2. Fourier Synthesis

Building Waves from Sinusoids

What you're seeing

Individual sinusoidal harmonics (grey) sum to produce the target waveform (gold). Add more harmonics to see the approximation improve. The Gibbs phenomenon (overshoot at discontinuities) never disappears — it's a fundamental property of Fourier convergence at jumps.

So what?

This is the core insight of Fourier analysis: ANY signal is a sum of sinusoids. A square wave needs infinite harmonics to be perfect; with 30, it's already very close. This is why bandwidth-limited signals (finite harmonics) are always smooth — they literally cannot have sharp edges.

3. Convolution Visualised

Flip, Shift, Multiply, Sum

What you're seeing

Top: input signal x[n] (blue) and flipped/shifted impulse response h[n-k] (red). Middle: element-wise product (green area = output value at current position). Bottom: output y[n] building up as the kernel slides across.

So what?

Convolution is the ONLY operation needed to compute any LTI system's output. Every filter, every reverb, every blur is this operation. Understanding the flip-shift-multiply-sum mechanics makes filter behaviour intuitive — you can SEE why a wider kernel means more smoothing.

4. Poles, Zeros & Frequency Response

Drag Poles and Zeros

What you're seeing

Left: the z-plane with unit circle. Poles (×) create peaks in the frequency response; zeros (○) create nulls. Right: the resulting magnitude frequency response, computed from the pole-zero positions. Drag them and watch the response reshape in real time.

So what?

This builds intuition for filter design. A pole near the unit circle at angle θ creates a resonance at frequency θ. A zero on the circle kills that frequency completely. Moving a pole outward (toward |z|=1) sharpens the peak; pulling it inward broadens it. This is how parametric equalisers work.

5. Window Functions & Spectral Leakage

See How Windows Shape the Spectrum

What you're seeing

Top: the windowed signal in time (window shape × sinusoid). Bottom: its magnitude spectrum (DFT). When the signal frequency lands exactly on a bin, the spectrum is clean. Move it between bins and watch leakage spread energy to neighbours. Different windows trade main-lobe width for side-lobe suppression.

So what?

This is why window choice matters for spectral analysis. The rectangular window gives best resolution but worst leakage (-13 dB side lobes). Blackman kills leakage (-58 dB) but widens the main lobe. There's no free lunch — you choose based on whether resolution or dynamic range matters more for your application.
