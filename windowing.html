The Leakage Problem

Why Finite Signals Leak

The DFT assumes the N-sample input repeats forever. If the signal doesn't contain an exact integer number of cycles within N samples, the assumed periodic extension has discontinuities at the boundaries. These discontinuities introduce energy at frequencies not present in the original signal — "spectral leakage." A pure 1000 Hz tone analysed with a non-integer number of cycles will appear to have energy smeared across many bins.

Imagine cutting a strip from an infinite wallpaper pattern. If you cut at exactly one repeat length, the strip tiles perfectly — no seam visible. But cut at a random point and the pattern mismatches at the edges when tiled. Those edge mismatches ARE spectral leakage — fake high-frequency content created by the abrupt cut, not present in the original pattern.

Window Functions: Controlling Leakage

The Windowing Solution

A window function w[n] multiplies the signal before the DFT, tapering it smoothly to zero at both ends. This eliminates boundary discontinuities — but at a cost: the tapered signal has less frequency resolution (wider main lobe). Every window is a trade-off between:

  • Main lobe width: Determines frequency resolution — how close two tones can be and still be distinguished
  • Side lobe level: Determines dynamic range — how much a strong tone leaks into neighbouring bins, potentially masking weak signals

The Kaiser Window

The Kaiser window is uniquely useful because it has an adjustable parameter β that continuously trades between rectangular (β=0, narrowest main lobe) and extreme taper (β>10, very low side lobes). This lets you design the window to match your application's specific resolution/dynamic-range needs. Jim Kaiser derived it as a computationally efficient approximation to the optimal (prolate spheroidal) window.

w[n] = 0.5(1 - cos(2πn/(N-1))),   0 ≤ n ≤ N-1 Hann window — the default choice in most applications

The Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT)

Time-Frequency Analysis

A single DFT tells you what frequencies exist in a signal but not WHEN they occur. The STFT solves this by computing the DFT on short, overlapping windows that slide through the signal. Each window gives a frequency snapshot at a specific time. Stacking these snapshots creates a spectrogram — a 2D image of time vs. frequency vs. amplitude.

X[m, k] = ∑n=0N-1 x[n + mH] · w[n] · e-j2πkn/N STFT: window position m, frequency bin k, hop size H

Parameters

  • Window size N: Larger = better frequency resolution but worse time resolution
  • Hop size H: Distance between successive windows. H = N/4 (75% overlap) is common for smooth spectrograms
  • Window type: Hann is standard; must satisfy COLA (Constant Overlap-Add) condition for perfect reconstruction

The STFT is like reading a book with a magnifying glass. A large magnifier (long window) lets you see many words clearly (good frequency resolution) but you can only focus on one area at a time (poor time resolution). A small magnifier (short window) lets you scan quickly (good time resolution) but each view shows fewer details (poor frequency resolution). You can't escape this trade-off — it's baked into the mathematics.

The Uncertainty Principle

Time-Frequency Resolution Limit

You cannot have arbitrarily good resolution in both time AND frequency simultaneously. This is the Gabor limit — the signal processing analogue of Heisenberg's uncertainty principle. The product of time resolution Δt and frequency resolution Δf is bounded:

Δt · Δf ≥ 1/(4π) Gabor uncertainty principle (Gaussian window achieves equality)

Practical Implications

For a 44.1 kHz audio signal with a 1024-sample window: frequency resolution = 44100/1024 ≈ 43 Hz, time resolution = 1024/44100 ≈ 23 ms. Want to resolve individual piano notes (needing ~10 Hz resolution)? You need ~4400 samples = 100 ms windows. But a 100 ms window can't distinguish events closer together than 100 ms — you'll miss rapid note onsets.

Beyond the STFT

Multi-Resolution Approaches

The STFT uses the same window size everywhere — same resolution at all frequencies. But many signals need variable resolution: long windows for low frequencies (which have long periods) and short windows for high frequencies (which change quickly). Solutions include:

  • Wavelet transform: Uses short windows at high frequencies, long at low — constant relative bandwidth (octave bands)
  • Constant-Q transform: Frequency bins logarithmically spaced (matching musical pitch) with Q = f/Δf constant
  • Reassignment methods: Sharpen the STFT by moving energy to its true centre of gravity within each time-frequency cell

Spectrograms in Practice

The Workhorse of Audio Analysis

Spectrograms are everywhere: speech recognition (mel spectrograms → neural networks), music analysis (pitch tracking, instrument identification), bioacoustics (whale song, bird identification), vibration monitoring (bearing fault signatures), and communications (signal identification, interference detection). The mel spectrogram — a spectrogram with frequency warped to match human pitch perception — is the standard input to modern speech and music AI systems.

The STFT's fixed time-frequency grid may be fundamentally mismatched to how audio signals are actually structured. Music lives on a log-frequency axis (octaves), speech has time-varying formant rates, and transients are impulse-like events that defy windowed analysis entirely. Learned time-frequency representations — where a neural network discovers the optimal decomposition for a downstream task — are beginning to outperform hand-designed transforms in some applications (SampleRNN, LEAF filterbanks). Are we approaching the end of the era where humans design signal decompositions, and entering one where they're learned from data?

Learned representations currently excel in classification tasks but lack the mathematical guarantees (perfect reconstruction, parsimony, interpretability) that make classical transforms trustworthy in safety-critical systems.

  • Can sparsity-promoting transforms (like those used in compressed sensing) be computed as efficiently as the FFT?
  • How should spectral analysis tools adapt for non-stationary signals where the statistics change faster than a single window length?
