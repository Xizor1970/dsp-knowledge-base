Two Families of Filters

The Fundamental Choice

Every digital filter falls into one of two families. FIR filters use only feedforward paths — the output depends only on current and past inputs. IIR filters add feedback — the output also depends on previous outputs. This single architectural decision ripples through every aspect of the filter's behaviour: phase response, stability, computational cost, and implementation complexity.

FIR Filters

y[n] = ∑k=0M bk · x[n-k] = b₀x[n] + b₁x[n-1] + ... + bMx[n-M] FIR filter: M+1 taps, no feedback

Characteristics

  • Always stable: No feedback means no poles outside the unit circle (all poles at z=0)
  • Linear phase possible: Symmetric coefficients give exactly linear phase — zero phase distortion
  • Exact implementation: No recursion, no accumulated rounding errors, no limit cycles
  • Higher order required: Typically 10–100× more taps than an equivalent IIR for the same magnitude response
  • Latency: Linear-phase FIR has group delay of (M-1)/2 samples

Typical Tap Counts

Application Taps Why
Simple smoothing 5–15 Gentle roll-off sufficient
Audio equaliser (linear-phase) 128–512 Sharp bands, no phase distortion
Sample rate converter 64–256 per phase High stopband rejection
Convolution reverb 48,000–96,000+ 1–2 second room impulse at 48 kHz

An FIR filter is like a moving spotlight on a conveyor belt. At each tick, you shine the spotlight on the most recent M items, assign a weight to each based on its position, multiply, and sum. The beam never looks backward at old totals — only at the raw items on the belt. Simple, predictable, but you need a wide beam (many taps) to be very selective.

IIR Filters

y[n] = ∑k=0M bkx[n-k] - ∑k=1N aky[n-k] IIR filter: feedback from previous outputs

Characteristics

  • Efficiency: Achieves sharp cutoffs with far fewer coefficients (a 4th-order IIR ≈ 800-tap FIR)
  • Potential instability: Feedback means poles can lie outside the unit circle if coefficients are wrong
  • Non-linear phase: Generally, IIR filters distort the phase relationship between frequencies
  • Infinite impulse response: The output of an IIR to an impulse rings forever (exponentially decaying)
  • Fixed-point issues: Quantisation of coefficients and intermediate values can cause limit cycles and overflow

The Biquad: Universal Building Block

Second-Order Sections

The biquad (bi-quadratic) is a 2nd-order IIR filter with 5 coefficients. It's the universal building block for IIR implementation because:

  • Any higher-order IIR is factored into cascaded biquads (reducing sensitivity to coefficient quantisation)
  • Complex pole/zero pairs naturally form 2nd-order sections
  • Parametric equalisers, crossovers, and tone controls are single biquads
  • Hardware DSPs optimise for biquad computation
H(z) = (b₀ + b₁z⁻¹ + b₂z⁻²) / (1 + a₁z⁻¹ + a₂z⁻²) Biquad transfer function — 5 coefficients define the complete filter

The biquad is to audio DSP what the LEGO brick is to construction. A single brick isn't much — it gives you one resonant peak or notch. But snap six together in series and you have a precision 12th-order crossover. The beauty is that each brick is small enough to understand completely, and the cascade is just the product of their transfer functions.

FIR vs. IIR: The Trade-off

Criterion FIR IIR
Stability Always stable Can be unstable
Phase Exactly linear (symmetric) Non-linear (generally)
Order for sharp cutoff High (100s of taps) Low (4–12 coefficients)
Computation per sample M+1 multiplies M+N+1 multiplies (much fewer total)
Latency High (linear-phase: (M-1)/2) Low (typically < 1 ms)
Quantisation effects Benign Limit cycles, overflow possible
Adaptability Easy (LMS, RLS algorithms) Harder (stability constraints)

Concrete Example

Sharp lowpass filter: 1 kHz cutoff, 48 kHz sample rate, 80 dB stopband attenuation, 100 Hz transition band:

  • FIR: ~800–1,000 taps. At 48 kHz, that's 800 multiplications per sample = 38.4 million multiplies/second. Latency: ~8.3 ms.
  • IIR (elliptic): 8th–10th order = 16–20 coefficients. ~20 multiplies per sample = 960,000 multiplies/second. Latency: < 0.2 ms.
  • Ratio: 40–50× fewer multiplications for IIR. But the FIR has perfect linear phase.

Implementation Structures

Direct Form I

Implements the difference equation as written: separate delay lines for input and output. Uses 2(M+N) delay elements. Straightforward but requires more memory.

Direct Form II (Transposed)

Shares delay elements between feedforward and feedback paths. Uses only max(M,N) delays — half the memory. This is the standard biquad implementation.

Cascade (Series) Form

Factors H(z) into a cascade of 2nd-order sections (biquads). Each section is implemented independently. Benefits: lower coefficient sensitivity, better numerical behaviour, easier tuning of individual frequency bands.

Parallel Form

Decomposes H(z) via partial fractions into parallel 2nd-order sections whose outputs are summed. Benefits: each section can saturate independently without affecting others — useful in fixed-point implementations.

Linear Phase

Why Phase Matters

A linear-phase filter delays all frequencies by the same amount (constant group delay). This preserves the shape of waveforms passing through — critical for audio (preserves transients), data communications (preserves pulse shape), and medical signals (preserves ECG morphology). Non-linear phase means different frequencies arrive at different times, smearing transients.

FIR filters achieve linear phase by making coefficients symmetric: b[k] = b[M-k]. This constraint halves the number of free parameters but guarantees zero phase distortion.

The FIR/IIR dichotomy is clean in theory but blurs in practice. "Truncated IIR" designs run an IIR for a fixed number of steps then zero the state — getting IIR-like efficiency with bounded-time behaviour. Conversely, "warped FIR" filters use allpass-chain delay elements to get variable frequency resolution while maintaining FIR stability. As processing power becomes less constrained, is the computational advantage of IIR still relevant? On modern hardware, a 1024-tap FIR at 48 kHz is trivial. The real battleground may shift from "how few multiplies?" to "how low can we make latency while maintaining perceptual quality?"

For embedded/battery-powered DSP (hearing aids, IoT sensors), the IIR efficiency advantage remains critical. Power consumption scales linearly with MAC (multiply-accumulate) operations, making the 50:1 ratio still meaningful at microwatt scales.
