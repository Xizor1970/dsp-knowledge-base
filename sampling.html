The Sampling Theorem

The Most Important Theorem in DSP

If a continuous-time signal x(t) contains no frequencies higher than fmax Hz, then it can be perfectly reconstructed from samples taken at a rate fs ≥ 2fmax. This minimum rate — 2fmax — is the Nyquist rate. Below it, information is irreversibly lost.

Harry Nyquist established the concept in 1928; Claude Shannon proved it rigorously in 1949 and showed how reconstruction works via the sinc interpolation formula.

fs ≥ 2 · fmax Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem
x(t) = ∑n=-∞∞ x[n] · sinc((t - nT)/T) Shannon's reconstruction formula (sinc interpolation)

Imagine filming a spinning wheel. If you take 24 frames per second and the wheel spins at 12 revolutions per second, you need at least 24 fps to see the motion correctly (each revolution needs at least 2 frames). Film at fewer fps and the wheel appears to spin backwards — that's aliasing. The Nyquist theorem tells you exactly how many "frames" you need to capture a signal without visual illusions.

Why 44.1 kHz?

The CD Standard

Human hearing ranges from roughly 20 Hz to 20 kHz. Applying Nyquist: we need at least 40,000 samples per second. The CD standard chose 44,100 Hz — slightly above the minimum — to allow room for a practical anti-aliasing filter's transition band (the filter can't cut off infinitely sharply at 20 kHz). The extra 2,050 Hz on each side gives the filter room to roll off from passband to stopband.

The specific number 44,100 also had a practical origin: it's evenly divisible by common video frame rates (44100 = 2² × 3² × 5² × 7²), which mattered when early digital audio was stored on video tape.

Aliasing: When Sampling Goes Wrong

Spectral Folding

When you sample below the Nyquist rate, high-frequency components "fold back" into the lower-frequency region, masquerading as frequencies that weren't in the original signal. A 15 kHz tone sampled at 20 kHz appears as a 5 kHz tone. The original frequency is gone — aliasing is irreversible.

Mathematically, sampling multiplies the signal's spectrum by a train of impulses spaced at fs. This replicates the spectrum at every multiple of fs. If the original spectrum exceeds fs/2, these replicas overlap — that overlap is aliasing.

falias = |fsignal - k · fs|,   where k = round(fsignal/fs) Aliased frequency calculation

Aliasing is like a photograph of a brick wall taken from a moving car. If the camera's shutter speed doesn't "sample" fast enough, the repeating pattern of bricks creates strange moiré patterns — fake low-frequency stripes that aren't on the actual wall. The true high-frequency detail (individual bricks) has folded into a phantom low frequency (the moiré bands).

Anti-Aliasing Filters

Prevention, Not Cure

Since aliasing is irreversible once sampling occurs, we must prevent it BEFORE the ADC. An anti-aliasing filter is a lowpass filter placed before the sampler that removes all energy above fs/2. In practice, this means the filter must transition from passband to full stopband within the narrow gap between the highest desired frequency and fs/2.

For CD audio: pass everything below 20 kHz, reject everything above 22.05 kHz. That's only a 2,050 Hz transition band — tight enough to require a high-order analogue filter (typically 7th–9th order elliptic).

Oversampling: The Modern Solution

Modern ADCs solve the steep-filter problem by oversampling: sampling at a much higher rate (e.g., 64× or 256× the final rate), then using a digital decimation filter to reduce back to the target rate. Because the initial sample rate is so high, the analogue anti-aliasing filter can be gentle (a simple first-order RC filter suffices). The hard work shifts to the digital domain where sharp filters are cheap and precise.

An anti-aliasing filter is like a velvet rope at a concert venue. You can't un-crowd a mosh pit once everyone's inside (aliasing), but you CAN check tickets at the door (filter) and only let in the frequencies that belong. Oversampling is like making the venue 64× larger — now a simple rope is enough because there's so much extra space that nobody crowds the wrong section.

Reconstruction

From Samples Back to Continuous

Shannon's interpolation formula says: place a sinc function at each sample, scale it by the sample value, and sum. The infinite tails of each sinc fill in the gaps between samples perfectly. In practice, we use a DAC (Digital-to-Analogue Converter) followed by a reconstruction lowpass filter that smooths the staircase output into the continuous signal.

Modern DACs also use oversampling: they upsample the signal digitally (inserting zeros and filtering), then convert at a high rate, making the analogue reconstruction filter trivial.

sinc(x) = sin(πx) / (πx) The sinc function — ideal interpolation kernel

Quantisation

The Other Sampling Decision

Sampling discretises time; quantisation discretises amplitude. An N-bit ADC maps the continuous voltage range into 2N discrete levels. A 16-bit converter (CD standard) has 65,536 levels — a dynamic range of about 96 dB. A 24-bit converter gives 16,777,216 levels and 144 dB of theoretical dynamic range.

SNRquantisation ≈ 6.02N + 1.76 dB Quantisation noise floor for N-bit uniform quantiser

Quantisation is like measuring height with a ruler that only has markings every centimetre. A person who is 175.3 cm gets rounded to 175 cm — the 0.3 cm error is quantisation noise. More bits is like adding millimetre markings: finer resolution, less rounding error, more faithful representation.

Bit Depth Levels Dynamic Range Typical Use
8-bit 256 ~48 dB Telephony, old games
16-bit 65,536 ~96 dB CD audio
24-bit 16.7M ~144 dB Professional recording
32-bit float ~4.3B ~1,528 dB Internal processing

Sigma-delta (ΣΔ) modulation has become the dominant ADC architecture for precision applications, trading bit depth for extreme oversampling rates (1-bit at 64× to 256× the output rate). As clock speeds continue to increase, the boundary between analogue and digital blurs further. Could future ADCs operate at optical clock rates, directly sampling terahertz signals? The information-theoretic limits are clear (Shannon's formula), but the engineering challenges of jitter, power, and thermal noise at those speeds remain formidable.

Current state-of-the-art ADCs achieve ~6 GSPS at 12-bit resolution. Terahertz-rate sampling remains theoretical, limited by aperture jitter (~50 femtoseconds in best devices) and fundamental thermal noise floors.
